The experiment

The question: to what extent is mirror-trading a profitable investment strategy in current-event prediction markets? The test: follow the people who are visibly winning, and see if following them wins too.

The mirror rule

Watch the Top-10 wallets on Polymarket’s 30-day profit leaderboard. Every time one of them trades, buys and sells alike, we record a paper copy of that trade at the real market price at that moment. Sells are normalized into equivalent long positions, so everything is comparable.

The benchmark

A strategy is only “skill” if it beats something dumber. Ours is “always bet the favorite”: in the same market, at the same moment, just buy whichever outcome the market already says is more likely. No leaderboard required.

The scoring

Every open position is re-priced every hour from Polymarket’s order-book price history. Markets that resolve settle at $1 (right) or $0 (wrong). The edge is simply: mirror return − favorite return, in the same markets at the same times.

The edge curve

Hold every copied position for N hours, then compare. The green line is the mirror strategy, the gray line is the always-bet-the-favorite benchmark, and the gold line is the difference: the edge.

Testing the edge against luck

An average edge means nothing without error bars. We bootstrap-resample the data 10,000 times (seeded, reproducible) two ways, treating each market bet as a unit and then each wallet as a unit, and ask how often a no-edge world would produce numbers like these.

p-values by horizon

Bars below the dashed 0.05 line would count as statistically significant. Brick = significant, gray = not.

What would convince us

For the edge to be evidence of skill, its 95% confidence interval would need to sit entirely above zero, under both clusterings, at horizons long enough to actually trade on, and survive realistic trading costs.

Gross returns

No fees, no bid-ask spread, no price impact. Real copy-trading would do worse than these numbers, not better. That makes a null result here conservative.

Paper trading

No real money was placed. Entries use the market mid-price at the moment the copied trade printed; an actual order would often get a worse price.

Late entries

A trade copied near the end of the window can’t be marked at long horizons yet, so sample sizes shrink as the horizon grows, and the error bars widen with them.

Study 1: the historical test

Before the live experiment, we asked the past: among 151 active Polymarket wallets, does anyone predict outcomes better than a robot betting at the same market prices? Each wallet’s Brier score was compared to a price-null via a 10,000-shuffle luck test, corrected for multiple testing.

Not one trader cleared the bar, and the single best result was nowhere near it. If nobody demonstrably has skill, there is nobody whose trades are worth mirroring. Study 2 tested that implication live.

Methods & glossary

Plain-English definitions of every term on this page.

Prediction market

A market where you buy shares in an outcome (“Yes, the Dodgers win”). Shares pay $1 if the outcome happens, $0 if it doesn’t, so a 62¢ price is the crowd saying “62% likely.”

Mirror trade

Copying someone else’s trade: same market, same side, at the price available when their trade became visible. We mirrored every buy and sell of the Top-10 leaderboard wallets, with sells normalized into equivalent long positions.

The favorite (benchmark)

Whichever outcome the market currently prices above 50%. “Always bet the favorite” needs no skill and no leaderboard. If copying winners can’t beat it, the leaderboard signal is worthless.

Gross return

(price now ÷ price paid) − 1, before any fees, spreads, or slippage. Real-world results would be strictly worse.

Why we de-duplicate repeated fills

A wallet might fire 50 small orders into the same bet within minutes. Counting all 50 would let one enthusiastic wallet vote 50 times. We collapse everything to one “cell” per (wallet, market, side) before averaging, so each distinct bet counts once.

Bootstrap confidence interval

Resample the observed bets (or wallets) with replacement 10,000 times and recompute the mean each time. The middle 95% of those means is the confidence interval. If it contains zero, the data are consistent with no edge at all.

p-value

The probability of seeing an edge at least this large (in either direction) if the true edge were zero. Small (< 0.05) = surprising under pure luck. Ours mostly aren’t.
